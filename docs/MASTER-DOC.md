# FinBiz Master Doc

One doc, two parts — **Product Matrix** (what you fund and its terms) and **Scripts** (what you say live) — resting on a shared **Base** (the logic both stand on). Product Matrix and Scripts stay apart. Every fact lives in exactly one place; don't restate it elsewhere.

## BASE — the ground both parts stand on

### Company facts

- The company is **FinBiz / FinBiz Funding** only — never name any parent/holding entity.
- Advertised: funding **$5K–$10M** · approvals in **~1 hour** · same-day funding possible.
- Primary product is the **MCA**. Everything else is a pivot or relationship play (see Product Matrix).

### Qualify floor (single threshold)

**$15K+/mo revenue · 6+ months in business · 500+ FICO · revenue deposited into a business bank account.** Miss any one → not yet eligible. Work the **LIGHT** track in Scripts (credit repair / CCP / call back with stronger months).

### Contact rule

Always secure **both** the merchant's **cell and email** — the info we were handed can be wrong, and two channels means you can still reach him if one fails.

### The pipeline (the spine every call rides)

1. **Prospecting** — reach the owner / authorized signer. Killers: inactive Secretary of State, wrong EIN/SSN, another broker submits first.
2. **Discovery** — need + urgency; open with the CTA: send 3 months of statements today.
3. **Pre-qualify** — apply the floor above.
4. **Collection** — get the minimum file (below).
5. **Statement review** — run the 10-point inspection + MCA hard gates (below).
6. **Submission** — package a clean, accurate file; confirm internal owner + deadline.
7. **Approval** — review terms; confirm product, factor rate, term, payment.
8. **Offer pitch & pivot** — present the fit; pivot via Product Matrix; handle objections.
9. **Contracts** — signature from an authorized signer; review total payback; no obligation to accept.
10. **Closing stips** — clear conditions.
11. **Funding** — confirm the wire.
12. **Follow-up** — renewal path (eligible ~50% paid down), referrals, restructure.

### Minimum file (collection)

- **Core (always):** completed application + owner ID / driver's license · EIN & ownership % · 3 months bank statements · voided check & business address · merchant cell + email.
- **Conditional:** payoff letters / bank logins · processing statements · current loan/advance balances.
- **Past default (since paid off):** require a **ZBL (Zero Balance Letter)** from the merchant, on top of bank statements and the product's docs.
- **Tax returns:** only for bank-funded products (e.g. SBA). Not for MCA. Confirm bank vs. non-bank before asking.

### 10-point statement review (healthy vs. red flag)

1. **Period** — recent/complete vs. gapped
2. **Name** — entity match vs. wrong owner
3. **Monthly deposits** — real revenue vs. transfers
4. **Deposit count** — active vs. sparse
5. **Average balance** — cushion vs. near zero
6. **Negative days** — stable vs. repeated negatives
7. **NSFs** — clean vs. failed payments
8. **ACH pulls** — single vs. stacking pressure
9. **Withdrawals** — business purpose vs. irregular
10. **Authenticity** — format match vs. manipulation

### MCA hard gates (the three small checks that actually decide it)

1. **Deposits:** average of **at least 3 deposits/month** across the last 3 months.
2. **Negative days:** **no more than ~4–5 negative days/month** on average across the last 3 months.
3. **Ending balance:** **positive ending balance in each of the last 3 months.** (Edge case: two amazing months + one slightly negative is workable to pitch, but funders still often reject it.)

### Risk signals that override the numbers

Frequent NSFs · stacked advances · negative balances · **prior default** · **modified positions** · unresolved liens/judgments · document refusal · ownership mismatch.

- **Modified position** = the merchant got unsure he could keep paying his daily/weekly MCA, so he had the funder restructure the remaining balance. To funders, modifying is **almost as bad as defaulting.**

## PART 1 — PRODUCT MATRIX (what you fund · which fits)

### Routing (read this first)

- **Good cash flow → MCA / LOC.**
- **Good credit (650+) → HELOC / Long-Term Loan / SBA.** HELOC also needs real-estate equity.
- **When the merchant fits, push HELOC over Term Loan / SBA** — it's faster (often same-day) and far less paperwork.
- **Term Loan / SBA only if he insists and nothing else fits.** They're heavy and slow — offer a **bridge loan** to fund him while it processes.

**MCA (Merchant Cash Advance) — PRIMARY**

- Best for: urgent working capital; active deposits, credit-flexible, needs speed.
- Vertical fit: funders pay for **predictable, stable cash flow.** A butcher with steady flow beats a real-estate agent who earns more but swings with seasonality. Steady wins.
- Funder appetite — hard spots: **Trucking** — funders dislike it; if they fund at all, it's almost always only when he already has a position open elsewhere. **Texas** — few funders fund there.
- Terms: factor rate + total payback (e.g. $20K × 1.40 = $28K; ~100 × $280/day). Non-bank — no tax returns. Daily/weekly payments.
- Weekly payments are NOT available if: (1) he has an open MCA position paid **daily**, or (2) **FICO under 650.** Otherwise daily is the default and weekly is the upsell.
- Say it: purchase of future receivables; factor rate + total payback. Never "interest," "APR," or "bank loan."

**Bridge Loan** (technically an MCA)

- What it is: an MCA with an **early-cashout clause** so the merchant can cash out the day his SBA / term loan funds. You call it a "bridge loan" because that's what it does in this spot — but it's an MCA underneath.
- When: he's set on a slow product (SBA / term loan) and needs capital now.

**Term Loan**

- Best for: only when the merchant is stubborn on it specifically and isn't a fit for anything else. Stronger-credit owner, predictable need, room for monthly.
- Reality: bureaucratically heavy and slow to fund. Offer a **bridge loan** in the meantime.
- Terms: from ~4.99% APR · $10K–$10M · 1–10 yr monthly · no prepay penalty.
- Say it: APR is fine here — it's a real loan. Rate depends on underwriting.

**Line of Credit**

- Best for: flexible, recurring needs; cleaner profile. (Cash-flow side, like MCA.)
- Terms: $250K–$500K revolving · draw and repay as needed.
- Say it: revolving — you only pay for what you draw.

**HELOC**

- Best for: **650+ FICO AND real-estate equity.** That's the whole test — bank statements and the rest don't matter here.
- Funding: often **within a day.**
- Power play — rescue a default: if a merchant is in **current default** with **650+ FICO and equity**, sell HELOC to pay the default off — so he closes it out instead of having to modify his position.
- Terms: in-house indicative quote (amount + rate) in ~5 min · secured against his real estate.
- Say it: a line against your property's equity; quick indicative number up front, final terms confirm against equity and title. Big draw: speed.

**Equipment Financing**

- What it is: a business loan or lease to acquire machinery, vehicles, technology, or furniture without paying the full cost upfront. The **equipment is the collateral**, so it's easier to qualify for than an unsecured loan.
- Reality: **very** bureaucratically exhausting for both the SDR and the merchant. Steer him to an alternative that fits his situation where you can.
- Terms: up to 100% financing · up to 7-yr terms.
- Say it: financed against the equipment. Possible write-offs — confirm with his CPA.

**Renewal / Refi / Consolidation**

- Best for: existing or prior-funded client; relief from daily pulls; clean up stacks.
- Terms: eligible ~50% paid down · fresh capital · consolidate obligations.
- Say it: better terms once paid down — and breathing room from daily payments.

**Asset-Based & Specialty** (invoice factoring / SBA / CRE)

- Best for: a specific asset or elite credit. SBA only when he insists — heavy and slow; offer a **bridge loan** meanwhile.
- Terms: factoring on receivables; SBA/CRE for top profiles.
- Say it: SBA is bank-funded — needs tax returns. Confirm bank vs. non-bank before asking for returns.

**CCP (Card Processing) — RELATIONSHIP PLAY, NEVER AN OPENER**

- Best for: holding an already-funded or not-yet-eligible merchant.
- Say it: never lead with it; use mid-conversation to keep the door open.

**Credit Repair — RELATIONSHIP PLAY, NEVER AN OPENER**

- Best for: converting a not-yet-eligible prospect into a future deal.
- Terms: up to $6K (scales with score) · 60–90 days to take effect.
- Say it: never guarantee a specific score or outcome.

**Structuring play — multi-entity routing**

When one owner controls two related companies and one can't qualify (or only on bad terms), fund through the commonly-owned entity with the stronger profile. Guardrails: the application names the true borrowing entity, accurate ownership, real revenue. Never inflate deposits via inter-company transfers; never misstate ownership.

**Rails:** no "guaranteed" · no rate before the file.

## PART 2 — SCRIPTS (what you say, live)

**Posture (how you carry all of it):** make him feel like a sharp businessman — respect his time and judgment, never talk down. At the same time, be the domain pro: precise, in command of the numbers. Target: a peer who happens to be the expert.

**Written follow-ups (SMS / email) — wording rail:** **never write the word "MCA"** — it carries a bad connotation. Call it **"funding"** (or similar). He can call it an MCA; you don't, in writing.

**Always get both channels:** before you hang up, lock his **cell and email** — the lead info can be wrong and you want two ways back to him.

**Discovery must surface** (before you advance): monthly revenue · time in business · current debt/funder balance · payment schedule on existing advances · use of funds · urgency · risk history (NSFs, liens, bankruptcies, defaults, modified positions). Then settle: eligibility · document-speed expectation · funds purpose · debt risk · defined next step.

### ① Open — set the frame

- "Hi ___, it's Ness. I work with owners in your line putting money to work in the business. Five minutes — worth it or not, I'll tell you flat."
- "I work with owners like you on growth capital — and I'm good at it."
- "Give me five minutes. By the end, either I see a real way to put money to work in your business, or I don't — and I'll tell you flat. Fair?"

### ② Open the story

- "If money wasn't the bottleneck, what's the first move you'd make?"

### ③ Gate (qualify against the floor)

- "So I'm not wasting your time — what's the business doing monthly recently, and how long have you been open?"
- Listen for the floor: $15K+/mo, 6+ months. Credit and bank account come with the statements.

### ④ Dig

- "Best time to grab capital is before you're desperate — money's cheapest when you don't need it, and you're in a good spot to move now. What's the one thing holding you back from the next level?"
- "How long's that been going on?"
- "If it stays like that another six months — what's it cost you? Jobs turned down, customers lost?"
- "And if it were handled tomorrow — what changes?" (He just gave you the pain and the payoff. Don't pitch yet.)

### ④.5 Risk check (mid / advanced stage)

- "Real quick so I build this right — any current defaults, or any position you've had to modify or restructure?"
- If he's defaulting or thinking about modifying → "What's your credit score sitting at?" If he's HELOC-eligible (650+ and owns real estate), pivot to HELOC to close the default out — so he never has to modify a position.

### ⑤ Point it + paint it

- "Here's what I'd put in front of you — a [pull the fit from Product Matrix]."
- "Picture it: [the thing he said he'd do], funded and moving. That's the play."
- "Depending on your numbers I'll get you the best terms I can — and I carry the paperwork. You run your business, I run this."

### ⑥ Close — hard, clean

- "Next: send me your last three months of bank statements today, I build your offer tonight, we talk tomorrow with real numbers."
- "This your cell? Texting you now." → "Hey, it's Ness — FinBiz." → "Shoot me your email."
- "No obligation — you see the full payback and you decide. Let's get the options on the table."

### ↪ All set — push for the second swing (refi/renewal)

- "Glad you're moving. Who'd you go with — and are payments daily, weekly, monthly?"
- "If those are daily pulls, I can probably refinance you into more breathing room. Want me to take a real look?"
- "And the day you hit about halfway paid, that's a top-up at better terms. I'll be the call you make — costs you nothing to have me in your corner."

### ↪ Light — not yet eligible, don't waste the call

- "Straight with you — numbers are a little light today for the best products. But I'm not writing you off."
- Offer one: credit repair to get eligible (60–90 days; never promise a score) · CCP to hold the line · "Give me two stronger months and a callback."

### ⚡ Objections

- "What's your rate?" → "Smart question — and I won't make up a number to keep you on the phone. The statements set the rate. Send them and I'll quote you real, today."
- "Not interested." → "Fair. Quick — is it timing, or you just don't see the need right now?" (reframe whichever)
- "Too expensive." → "Compared to what? If the money makes you more than it costs, the price answers itself."
- "Bad credit." → "That's fine, honestly. Revenue does the heavy lifting here, not the score. Let's pull the statements and see."
- HELOC — "I don't want to put my house/equity on the line." → "I hear you, and that's exactly why it's smart, not risky. This is the fastest money you can get — funded in about a day — and it's a **line**, not a lump you owe. You only draw what you need and you stay in control of it. It's there to solve the problem, not to be spent. Used right, it's the cheapest, fastest tool you've got."
